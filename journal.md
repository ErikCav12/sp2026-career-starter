# Part 1: Journal

## Who are you?

I studied mechanical engineering at Strathclyde. Spent semesters in Australia and France, which gave me a wider lens on how different industries and cultures approach engineering problems. I thought I was heading into aerospace R&D. I wasn't.

At Atkins I led R&D for an aerospace OEM, running 8-12 concurrent work packages. Technically interesting. But I kept ending up in the client conversations, trying to understand why things were being built, not just how. That pull toward the "why" is what eventually took me to Accenture.

### Fork 1: Atkins to Accenture

I chose Accenture for the pedigree and the variety. Different clients, different industries, different scales of problem. I wanted to understand how large organisations actually adopt technology, not just how to build it. I ended up leading a team of 20+ engineers across a major aerospace and defence digital transformation programme. That's where things got real.

### Fork 2: GenAI changes everything

Midway through my time at Accenture, GenAI arrived. I'd been working at the intersection of engineering and digital transformation for years, and this was different from anything I'd seen. Not a 2-3% optimisation. I was seeing 80%, 90% improvements in real workflows. I identified an opportunity to build a RAG chatbot for mechanical engineering design, pushed it through every review process (data, legal, security, a first for that client type), and shipped it to production. That experience made the direction obvious.

### Fork 3: Accenture to FractalTech

I left Accenture for a 12-week applied AI intensive. The decision was simpler than it looked from the outside: I wanted to build things, not advise on them. I wanted to own the outcome. Accenture had taught me how to deliver at scale, how to manage complexity, how to earn client trust. Valuable. But the day-to-day had become more process than building, and I was restless.

FractalTech was the opposite. Applied learning, shipping every week, working with startups in the AI space. RAG systems, agentic architectures, evals, full-stack development. Hands dirty from day one.

### What this tells me about myself

I'm drawn to building things that solve real problems for real people. Every move I've made has been toward more ownership and more visible impact. Engineering taught me how to think in systems. Delivery taught me how to scope and how to earn trust. AI is where both of those things actually get used together.

I get motivated by pressure and visible results. I get stuck when the work is purely process, when I'm managing rather than making. I avoid staying in comfort zones, sometimes to my own detriment.

The thread through all of it: I keep choosing the harder, more impactful path. Strathclyde to Atkins was safe. Atkins to Accenture was a step up. Accenture to FractalTech was a leap. Each time, I traded stability for growth, and each time, it compounded.

### Outside work

I captained Norway's rugby team. That matters because leadership under physical pressure is different from leadership in a meeting room. When the stakes are real and immediate, you learn who you actually are as a leader, not who you think you are.

### The hard question

The thing I'd rather people didn't probe too hard on: my AI experience is measured in months, not years. I've been building intensively since January 2026. I've shipped to production, built end-to-end systems, designed evals. It's real work. But it's recent, and I know that. I'm betting on learning velocity and a track record of delivering under pressure. That bet has paid off so far, but I'm aware that "so far" isn't the same as "proven."

## What can you do?

### The projects that matter

**Digital Thread Turnaround (Accenture)**
The workstream was considered failing. Twenty engineers, a skeptical client who openly questioned whether I was experienced enough, and leadership that had written off the possibility of a productionised solution. The real problem wasn't technical, it was trust. The team had been building in isolation, presenting finished solutions for rubber-stamping, running too many meetings with no tangible output. I changed the operating rhythm to complete transparency: shared priorities weekly, surfaced issues before they festered, brought client SMEs into the acceptance journey early. Within three months, things turned around. £30M ROI in the first year. The client asked me to take on programme leadership by month twelve. I'm most proud of this one. Everyone had decided it couldn't be done, and we did it anyway.

**RAG Chatbot for Defence (Accenture)**
Mechanical engineers were spending hours per component searching the same ten documents to build repeatable designs. I proposed a RAG solution, built the business case, and co-architected it alongside a data engineer. This was the first GenAI project pushed through Accenture's full legal, data, and security review for this client type. Defence constraints meant open-source models only, no data leaving via API. The critical decision was building evaluations before the solution: the model was confidently misidentifying materials, and we only caught it because we had evals running from the start. Deployed to production, 80% reduction in engineering hours, still in use today.

**Training Glider Digital Twin (Atkins)**
Found a cupboard full of handwritten damage records at a Ministry of Defence site. Proposed a three-part digital transformation: OCR for the handwritten data, an interactive 3D model using game engine technology, and data linkage so each damage point connected to its full repair history. Assembled the team, ran client discovery workshops, managed the budget. Engineers went from searching physical cupboards to clicking markers on a 3D model. 20% reduced downtime, 40% fewer engineering hours.

**Scottish Golf AI Concierge (FractalTech)**
A contact in Scotland wanted to replace the manual tour operator workflow with an AI-powered itinerary planner. I designed and built the full stack in one week: frontend with a planning workflow, backend with API integrations to booking sites, and a ReAct agent loop where the LLM decides which tools to call (querying the course database, checking drive times, verifying availability). The architecture choice was deliberate: the LLM couldn't invent courses or fake availability because every claim was grounded in tool calls. Working MVP, positive user testing.

### Skills, honest assessment

Sharp right now: client-facing delivery, scoping AI projects from vague briefs, stakeholder management under pressure, RAG architecture, agentic system design, evals, full-stack (React, Python, TypeScript).

Developing: production ML infrastructure at scale, model training and fine-tuning, distributed systems.

Where I'd back myself against most people: turning an ambiguous client need into a shipped solution. Building trust with someone who doesn't trust me yet. Scoping ruthlessly. The RAG chatbot worked because I chose the ten documents that covered 80% of use cases instead of trying to boil the ocean. The golf concierge worked because I forced every claim through tool calls, not because the model was clever. Knowing what to leave out is the skill that took the longest to learn and matters the most.

The gap between paper and reality: on paper, I look like a delivery manager who recently picked up AI. In practice, I'm someone who spent years learning how to deliver at scale and is now building with all of that context. The golf concierge (full stack, one week, working product) doesn't fit neatly onto a CV the way "managed 20 engineers" does. But it's a better signal of what I actually do with my time.

## What can you offer right now?

If someone hired me tomorrow, they'd get a person who can walk into a client meeting, understand the problem, figure out where AI fits (and where it doesn't), scope the solution, build the business case, and lead delivery through to production. I've done this repeatedly across different contexts.

My edge: I can do both sides. Most AI engineers can build but freeze in a client meeting. Most delivery leads can run a programme but can't write the code. I've done both, in the same role, on the same project, and shipped it to production in a regulated environment.

I make teams better by translating between technical and business conversations. I've sat in rooms where engineers couldn't explain their work to clients, and rooms where clients couldn't articulate what they needed to engineers. That gap is where I operate.

Limits, honestly: my AI-specific depth is months, not years. I'd need support on production ML infrastructure at scale and advanced model training. I'm fast but sometimes imprecise under pressure. I fumble specific numbers or technical terms when I'm nervous, and I have a tendency to keep talking past the point where I should stop. I know this about myself, and I'm actively working on it.

If you asked my last manager or closest collaborator what I'm like to work with, they'd probably say: works very hard, throws himself at problems, learns fast, good at building trust with clients. The honest version would also include: sometimes needs to slow down and be more methodical, and can get so focused on delivering that he doesn't step back and check direction.

## Where do you want to go?

The next chapter is applied AI. Building solutions for real clients, not internal tools or research projects. The title doesn't matter much to me. The work does: figuring out where AI fits, designing the system, building it, and getting it into production where it actually stays.

In a year: established as a credible AI architect delivering production AI systems. Known as someone who can own a client relationship and the architecture simultaneously.

In three years: leading an AI practice or team, or building an AI-native product company. The long game is becoming someone who shapes how organisations adopt AI, not just implementing it but defining what "good" looks like.

Environment: small to medium, fast-paced, high technical bar. Short delivery cycles where you see the impact of your work quickly. Not big corporate. I want to be around people who build and ship, not people who only talk about building and shipping.

The problems I want to work on: why most AI projects never make it to production. The distance between a working demo and a system someone actually trusts with real decisions. That's the problem I keep coming back to, and I think it's the one that matters most right now.

## How do you want to grow?

The gap between where I am and where I want to be is primarily depth. I have breadth (client delivery, scoping, architecture, full-stack, RAG, agents, evals). What I need is deeper experience running these systems at scale in production over months, not weeks. Seeing how they degrade, how to monitor them, how to evolve them as requirements change.

The next concrete thing I need to learn: building and running evaluation pipelines in production. Evals are the bridge between a prototype that looks good in a demo and a system you'd trust with real decisions. I've built evals for individual projects. I want to get to the point where I can design evaluation strategies that scale across a portfolio of AI systems.

The growth that matters most right now is going deeper technically while maintaining the client delivery strength. The temptation is to go fully technical and leave the client work behind, but that mix is what makes me different. Losing either half would make me less valuable.

The stretch that scares me: becoming the person clients ask for by name. Not "a good delivery lead" or "a solid engineer" but "the person who consistently ships AI that works." That means every project has to be good, not just most of them. That level of consistency is what I'm building toward.

## Where do you belong?

What I'm looking for in an organisation:
- Small to mid-size. I want my work to be visible and my contributions to matter, not disappear into a machine.
- Fast-paced with short delivery cycles. I do my best work when there's urgency and the feedback loop is tight.
- High technical bar. I want to be around people who are better than me at specific things, because that's how I learn fastest.
- Client-facing work. I don't want to sit behind an API. I want to understand the person using what I build.
- A team that values shipping over talking. Meetings about meetings is where I die inside.

Specific targets:
- Boutique AI consultancies that work with enterprise clients on production AI, where the engagement is weeks not years and the expectation is a working system at the end.
- AI-native product companies at the stage where they need someone who can both build the product and talk to customers.
- Any organisation where the culture is "build it, ship it, measure it, improve it" rather than "plan it, present it, approve it, plan it again."

What draws me to these environments: the commitment to putting AI into production, not just talking about it. Short cycles that force you to learn fast. Teams that are small enough that everyone matters and good enough that you have to earn your place every week.

The ideal opportunity: a role where I own both the client relationship and the technical solution. Where the work is scoping AI systems, building them, and making them better, with a team that holds itself to a high standard and moves fast. That's where I'd do my best work, and that's where I'd grow the fastest.
