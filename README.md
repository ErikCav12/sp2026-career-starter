# Fractal Tech Spring 2026 Career Starter

A structured guide for auditing where you stand in your career right now, clarifying where you're headed, and making sure the basics of how you show up in the world are covered.

Today we're going to start with the basics: who are you, what do you want, where are you going, and how do you show that to the world?

## Getting Started

1. Clone this repo.
2. Create a new **private** repo under your own GitHub account (e.g., `yourname/sp2026-career-starter`).
3. Change the remote to point to your new repo instead of this one:
   ```
   git remote set-url origin https://github.com/yourname/sp2026-career-starter.git
   git push -u origin main
   ```
4. Add [@liamdanielduffy](https://github.com/liamdanielduffy) and [@dsshimel](https://github.com/dsshimel) as collaborators on your repo so we can see your work.

## Part 1: Journaling

Spend at least an hour - more if you find it useful - journaling and writing up your best answers to the questions below. There's no wrong way to do this. You can journal with pen and paper and write up your thoughts in a `.md` file afterwards, or you can ask Claude to interview you on these questions and work through them conversationally. Whatever gets you thinking honestly. At the end of your session, commit artifacts representing the results of your journaling and reflection to this repo. They don't need to be polished - as freeform as you want.

### Who are you?

Tell your story. Where did you start, and where did you think you were going? What actually happened? Walk through the major forks in the road - the decisions that shaped the path you're on now.

Which experiences were the most meaningful? Which ones were dead ends? Where did you feel most alive, and where did you feel like you were going through the motions? What are you most proud of? Is there anything you'd rather people not ask about?

What does all of this tell you about yourself - what you're drawn to, what you're good at, what you avoid, and what kind of person you've become through it? What have you learned about how you work, what motivates you, and where you tend to get stuck?

### What can you do?

Talk about the work you've done. Pick the projects, roles, or contributions you're most proud of and go deep. What was the problem? What did you actually do - not your team, you? What was hard about it? What did you build, ship, fix, or figure out?

What skills have you stacked up along the way? Which ones are sharp right now, and which ones are rusty? Where are you genuinely strong - the things where you'd back yourself against most people? Where are you just okay? What's a skill you thought mattered that turned out not to, and what's one you undervalued that turned out to be critical?

If someone skeptical were reading your resume, what would make them stop and pay attention? What's the most impressive thing you've done that doesn't show up on paper? Where's the gap between how good you actually are and how good you look on paper - in either direction?

### What can you offer right now?

If someone hired you tomorrow, what would they be getting? What problems could you walk into and start solving on day one? What kind of team would you make better just by being on it, and why?

What's your edge - the thing you bring that most people in your position don't? Is it technical depth, taste, speed, reliability, communication, some specific domain knowledge? Be honest about what it actually is versus what you wish it were.

What are the limits of what you can offer today? Where would you need support, ramp-up time, or a longer leash? If someone asked your last manager or closest collaborator what you're like to work with, what would they say - the good and the real?

### Where do you want to go?

What does the next chapter look like? What kind of work do you want to be doing in a year? In three years? What does a career you'd be genuinely excited about look like - not the safe answer, the real one?

What kind of environment do you thrive in? Big company or small? Fast and scrappy or structured and stable? What kind of people do you want around you? What kind of problems do you want to spend your days on?

### How do you want to grow?

What's the gap between where you are and where you want to be? What skills, experiences, or knowledge do you need to close it? What's the next thing you need to learn - not in the abstract, but concretely, to get where you're going?

What kind of growth matters most to you right now - going deeper technically, learning to lead, broadening your range, building credibility in a new area? What's a stretch that scares you a little but would change your trajectory if you pulled it off?

### Where do you belong?

Given what you offer now and where you want to grow, what kind of organization would be the right fit? Think about it in terms of qualities - stage, size, pace, culture, mission, technical bar, how they treat their people. What kind of team would bring out your best work? What kind of work environment would actually help you grow in the ways you just described?

Are there specific companies, teams, or roles you already have in mind? Write them down, even if they feel like long shots. What draws you to each one? If nothing specific comes to mind yet, describe what the ideal opportunity looks like in enough detail that you'd recognize it when you see it.

## Part 2: The Elevator Pitch

Now take everything you just wrote and make it legible to someone else. You need to be able to explain yourself quickly and clearly - at a networking event, in an intro email, at the top of a call. If someone asks "so what do you do?" or "what are you looking for?", you shouldn't have to think about it.

Write four versions:

- **5 seconds** - One sentence. Who you are and what you're looking for. This is the version for when someone asks at a party.
- **30 seconds** - A few sentences. Your background, what you're good at, and what you want next. This is the version for a warm intro or the first minute of a call.
- **60 seconds** - A short paragraph. Enough to tell your story with some texture - where you've been, what you bring, where you're headed, and why it makes sense. This is the version for a cover letter or a "tell me about yourself."
- **5 minutes** - The full picture. Everything someone would need to understand you as a candidate - your trajectory, your strengths, your growth areas, what you're looking for, and why. This is the version you'd walk through with a recruiter or a mentor who's trying to help you.

These don't need to be perfect on the first pass. The point is to force yourself to prioritize - what actually matters when you only have a few seconds? What gets cut? What stays? Commit these to the repo alongside your journal.

## Part 3: The Audit

Your story shows up across multiple surfaces - LinkedIn, your personal website, your resume. These are all just different instantiations of the same underlying story. The whole point is telling that story honestly and authentically everywhere someone might find you. Right now, we're not fixing anything. We're just looking.

For each surface below, open it up and go through the checklist. For every item, note one of three things:

1. **Good** - It's there and it aligns with your story from Parts 1 and 2.
2. **Gap** - It's missing or empty.
3. **Misaligned** - It's there but it doesn't match your current story, skills, or direction.

Write your audit in a `.md` file with a section for each surface. Be specific - don't just say "needs work," say exactly what's wrong or missing. Commit it to the repo when you're done.

### LinkedIn

Open your LinkedIn profile and check:

- [ ] **Headline** - Should say what you do and what you're looking for. Not just a job title.
- [ ] **About section** - Should be your elevator pitch. Should answer "tell me about yourself." The audience is career-oriented - not Twitter, not Instagram. You have full license to go career mode here. Should show that you're a professional who can get the job done and is serious about doing good work. Should highlight at a glance: who you are, where you've been, what you offer, and what you're looking for. You don't need to differentiate on personality or well-roundedness. This is about competence and direction.
- [ ] **Experience descriptions** - Each role should have bullet points describing what you did. Not just a title and a company name.
- [ ] **Skills list** - Should reflect your actual strengths, not just keywords from five years ago.
- [ ] **Profile photo** - Should be professional enough that a recruiter wouldn't hesitate to forward your profile.

### Personal website

Open your personal website (or note that you don't have one - that's a gap).

- [ ] **Identity** - Should make clear that you're the person someone is looking for. Name, photo, enough context to confirm.
- [ ] **Contact** - Should have an obvious way to reach you (email, contact form).
- [ ] **Links to other surfaces** - Should point to LinkedIn, GitHub, etc.
- [ ] **Core story on the homepage** - Should answer who you are, what you do, and what you're looking for. Not just a name and a blank page.
- [ ] **Projects page** - Should have projects. For each project listed, check:
  - [ ] There should be a write-up explaining what it is and what you did.
  - [ ] There should be a live link.
  - [ ] There should be a link to the code.
- [ ] **Vibes check** - Should avoid these two failure modes:
  - Construction site - "under construction" banners, placeholder pages, half-built sections
  - Ghost town - empty pages, missing content zones, links that go nowhere

### Resume

Pull up your current resume.

- [ ] Should be one page.
- [ ] Content should match your story from Part 1 and your pitch from Part 2.
- [ ] Each bullet point should describe impact, not just responsibilities.
- [ ] For detailed resume advice and a Claude prompt to help you improve yours, see: [fractal-nyc/bootcamp-monorepo/advice/resume](https://github.com/fractal-nyc/bootcamp-monorepo/tree/main/advice/resume)

### GitHub

Open your GitHub profile.

- [ ] **Profile README** - Should have one. Should reinforce your story - who you are, what you're working on, what you're interested in.
- [ ] **Pinned repos** - Should be your best work. A hiring manager looking at them should understand what you can do.
- [ ] **Project READMEs** - Each pinned repo should have a README that explains what the project is, why it exists, and how to run it.

## Part 4: Close the Gaps

Now go back through your audit and start fixing things. Update your LinkedIn, your website, your resume, your GitHub - whatever needs it. Use what you wrote in Parts 1 and 2 as your source material.

The goal isn't just checking boxes. The goal is coherence. Every surface should be telling the same story - the one you figured out in your journal. Your LinkedIn about section, your resume summary, your website homepage, your GitHub profile README - these should all read like different formats of the same underlying narrative. If someone reads your LinkedIn and then clicks through to your personal site, it should feel like the same person wrote both. If a recruiter reads your resume and then looks at your GitHub, the picture should get richer, not more confusing.

No blank sections, no outdated descriptions, no profiles that contradict each other. Someone should be able to find you on any surface and come away with the same clear picture of who you are, what you can do, and what you're looking for.

Stretch goal: by the end of the day, every checkbox in Part 3 is checked off and every surface tells your story.
