# Part 2: Elevator Pitch

## 5 seconds

Chartered engineer turned AI architect. I scope, build, and ship production AI for enterprise clients.

## 30 seconds

I'm a chartered mechanical engineer who spent six years delivering complex technical programmes, leading 20+ engineers, turning around a project everyone had written off, and shipping £30M in first-year ROI. I moved into AI because I kept seeing how much of what businesses need never actually makes it to production. I've shipped a RAG system for a defence client (80% reduction in engineering hours) and built an AI-powered product from scratch in a week. I want a role where I own both the client relationship and the technical architecture.

## 60 seconds

I started in aerospace engineering: R&D, digital twins, complex multi-discipline programmes. At Accenture I led delivery for a major digital transformation, managing 20+ engineers across a programme that delivered £30M in first-year ROI. Along the way I spotted an opportunity to apply GenAI to mechanical engineering workflows, built the business case, and shipped a RAG chatbot to production on a defence client. That project taught me something fundamental: in environments where getting it wrong has real consequences, the evaluation framework is the product. The model is just the engine. You have to define what "good" looks like before you write a line of code.

That conviction is why I left Accenture for an intensive applied AI programme. Twelve weeks of building: RAG systems, agentic architectures, evaluation pipelines, shipping every week, working directly with startups. What I bring is both sides of the table: I've managed 20-person delivery teams and I've built full-stack AI products solo in a week. I want to be in a role where I do both, where understanding the client's problem and building the technical solution aren't two different jobs.

## 5 minutes

My career has followed a thread that only makes sense looking back: each move traded comfort for impact.

I studied mechanical engineering at Strathclyde, including international exchanges in Australia and France. Joined Atkins as an R&D lead for an aerospace OEM, managing 8-12 concurrent work packages. Technical depth, the traditional engineering track. Good work. But I kept noticing that the hardest problems weren't technical. They were about getting solutions adopted, building trust with clients, translating between what engineers built and what organisations actually needed.

That instinct pulled me to Accenture. The pedigree, the variety of clients, and the chance to work at a scale I couldn't get anywhere else. I ended up leading the engineering workstream on a major aerospace and defence digital transformation. 20+ engineers, a client who initially doubted I was experienced enough, a programme that leadership had written off. The workstream was considered failing when I took it on.

The real problem wasn't technical, it was trust. The team had been building in isolation, presenting finished solutions for rubber-stamping. I changed the operating rhythm to complete transparency, brought client SMEs into the design process early, and rebuilt the relationship from the ground up. Within three months, the turnaround was visible. £30M ROI in year one. The client asked me to take on programme leadership by month twelve. That project taught me how delivery actually works at scale, and it's the work I'm most proud of.

While embedded on that account, I spotted an opportunity to apply GenAI to mechanical engineering design. Engineers were spending hours per component searching the same ten documents. I proposed a RAG chatbot, built the business case, and co-architected the solution alongside a data engineer. Defence constraints meant open-source models only, nothing leaving via API. The critical decision was building evaluations before the solution existed: the model was confidently misidentifying materials, and we only caught it because we had evals running from day one. Shipped to production. 80% reduction in engineering hours. Still in use today.

That project changed how I think about AI delivery. The technology usually works fine. The problem is that nobody defines what "good" looks like before they start building. In high-stakes environments, the eval framework is the actual product. The model is just the engine. I've carried that forward into everything I've built since.

When GenAI started transforming every industry, I made a decision: I didn't want to advise on AI from a consulting seat. I wanted to build. I left Accenture and joined a 12-week intensive applied AI programme, building end-to-end systems every week. RAG architectures, agentic systems, evaluation pipelines, full-stack development. One of the projects I'm proudest of from that period: I built an AI-powered golf concierge in a single week. Full stack, API integrations, a ReAct agent loop that couldn't hallucinate because every factual claim was grounded in tool calls. Working MVP, positive user testing.

I'll be honest about where I am: my AI experience is months of intensive building, not years. I've shipped to production, designed evaluation frameworks, built agentic systems. But someone with five years of ML engineering has depth I don't have yet. What I offer instead is range. I can walk into a client meeting, figure out what they actually need, scope the AI solution, design the architecture, build it, and get it into production. Most people I meet can do one or two of those things well. Doing all of them is what I'm betting on.

I want a role where I own the client relationship and the technical solution, at a company that ships production AI on short timelines. Small to mid-size, high technical bar, a team that holds itself to a standard. The case for me is learning velocity and a delivery track record, not years on a CV. That bet has paid off every time I've made it, and I'm making it again.
